  1. Annual income for \(1\) full-time Utah minimum wage worker.
    1. Federal (and Utah) minimum wage is \(\$7.25\)/hour. Round to \(\$10\) to make the math easier (we only care about the number of zeroes in our answer, so this should turn out fine).
    2. A full-time worker works \(40\) hours/week.
    3. There are \(52\approx50\) weeks in a year.
    4. Thus the annual income is approximately
      \begin{equation*} \frac{\$10}{\text{hour}}\times\frac{40\text{ hours}}{\text{week}}\times\frac{50\text{ weeks}}{\text{year}}=\frac{\$20,000}{\text{year}}. \end{equation*}
      It’s in the tens of thousands of dollars. Notice how all the units cancel out and the answer is in dollars per year, as desired, while our given information was in dollars per hour. At each step, we’re converting from one unit to another (dollars/hour to dollars/week to dollars/year).
    5. Real answer (for federal/Utah minimum wage):
      \begin{equation*} \frac{\$7.25}{\text{hour}}\times\frac{2,080\text{ hours}}{\text{year}}=\$15,080 \end{equation*}
      also in the tens of thousands! Note how close our answer is. This is because our rounding (up and down) canceled itself out.

  7. Average tax rate for an American in the wealthiest \(1\%\text{.}\)
    1. We use the given facts; first, we note the average American in the wealthiest \(1\%\) earns \(\$2.5\) million per year, \(40\%\) of which (\(\$1\) million) is made in capital gains. This means \(\$1.5\) million is not made from capital gains and is taxed at the higher \(40\%\) tax rate.
    2. The tax on the capital gains portion of income is easier to estimate:
      \begin{equation*} 0.2(\$1,000,000)=\$200,000 \end{equation*}
    3. The tax rate on the non-capital gains income would be \(\$150,000\) plus \(40\%\) of the amount over \(\$500,000\text{.}\) Since the non-capital gains income totals \(\$1,500,000\text{,}\) the amount over \(\$500,000\) is \(\$1\) million. So the tax on the non-capital gains income would be
      \begin{equation*} \$150,000+0.4(\$1,000,000)=\$150,000+\$400,000=\$550,000. \end{equation*}
    4. Thus, the total tax paid by the average member of the \(1\%\) is \(\$550,000+\$200,000\text{.}\) To find the tax rate paid, we have to figure out what percent of their income this average \(1\)-percenter paid. They paid \(\$750,000\) in taxes, and their income was \(\$2.5\) million, so they paid a tax rate of
      \begin{equation*} \frac{\$750,000}{\$2,500,000}\times100\%=30\%. \end{equation*}

  12. # of Americans arrested each second for possession of weed alone from 2001-2010.
    1. We know about \(600,000\) Americans were arrested for weed alone in \(2010\text{.}\) Assuming weed policies were more lenient in \(2010\) than in \(2001\text{,}\) we’d guess maybe an average of at most \(1,000,000\) Americans a year over this \(10\)-year period, for a total of
      \begin{equation*} 10\times1,000,000=10,000,000 \end{equation*}
      Americans arrested.
    2. We now estimate seconds/year:
      \begin{equation*} 1\text{ year}\times\frac{350\text{ days}}{\text{year}}\times\frac{25\text{ hours}}{\text{day}}\times\frac{60\text{ min}}{\text{hr}}\times\frac{60\text{ sec}}{\text{min}}=30,000,000\text{ sec}. \end{equation*}
    3. This means that there were
      \begin{equation*} \frac{10,000,000}{30,000,000}\approx0.3\text{ arrests/sec} \end{equation*}
      or about one arrest every \(3\) sec.
    4. Now note that multiple people could be swept up in a single pot bust. Assume \(2\) people on average per bust, that gives
      \begin{equation*} 0.3\frac{\text{arrests}}{\text{sec}}\times\frac{1\text{ bust}}{2\text{ arrests}}=0.15\frac{\text{busts}}{\text{sec}} \end{equation*}
      or \(15\) busts every \(100\) sec. This is on the same order of magnitude as the ACLU’s estimate, so their estimate is reasonable.
